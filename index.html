Steemit: The Chain That Paid for Brains

The first serious attempt to pay for content with a token. It pioneered the creator-economy primitives everyone now reuses — and demonstrated, in public and at scale, every way stake-weighted incentives can eat themselves.

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The whole story in five sentences

TL;DR

Steem (blockchain live March 2016; the Steemit front-end on July 4, 2016) tried to monetize social media at the protocol layer via "Proof-of-Brain": a rewards pool funded by inflation, paid out to content and curators by stake-weighted community voting.2

It genuinely pioneered the reusable building blocks of on-chain creator economies — a rewards pool, community curation, vesting stake (Steem Power), a stable-token (SBD), and a parameterized author/curator split — and in the early days it really did put crypto in creators' wallets.13

But the core rule was explicit — "the only way to increase influence is to increase stake" — and a superlinear reward curve amplified it, producing exactly the plutocracy critics predicted: whale-dominated voting, self-voting rings, and an industrial bid-bot economy that captured the reward pool.410

Steemit Inc spent three years patching the economics (linear curve in 2017; a 50/50 split, convergent-linear curve, and a proposal DAO in 2019) — fixes that shifted behavior more than they cured it.8

The defining failure was governance: in 2020 Justin Sun's Tron bought Steemit Inc and its founder stake, the community soft-forked to freeze it, Sun voted out the witnesses using customer funds borrowed from exchanges, and the community escaped by hard-forking into Hive — the canonical real-world proof that coin-voting is capturable.116

SECTION 1

Origins & design intent

Steem set out to answer a question Web2 never solved fairly: if a platform's value is created by its users, why do users capture none of it? Facebook and Reddit monetized attention; the people producing the content and doing the moderation got nothing. Steem's bet was that you could pay them — directly, in a token, at the protocol layer.

Launched in 2016 by Dan Larimer (already known for BitShares, later EOS) and Ned Scott, Steem ran as a Delegated Proof-of-Stake blockchain: token holders elect 21 "witnesses" to produce blocks, which is what made transactions fast and feeless — a prerequisite if every upvote, comment, and post is going to be an on-chain operation.1

The headline idea was "Proof-of-Brain." The name is a deliberate pun on Proof-of-Work: instead of burning electricity to distribute new tokens, Steem would distribute them to whoever the crowd judged to have done the most valuable human work — writing and curating. The bluepaper describes it as two mechanisms fused together: a rewards pool of newly minted tokens "dedicated to incentivizing content creation and curation," and "a voting system that leverages the wisdom of the crowd to assess the value of content."2

Steem operates on the basis of one-STEEM, one-vote… individuals who have contributed the most, as measured by their account balance, have the most influence… The only way for users to increase their influence is to increase their stake. — Steem Bluepaper. This sentence is the whole post-mortem in miniature.4

That design choice — votes weighted by money, not by people — was not an accident or an oversight. It was the explicit, load-bearing premise. Everything that worked and everything that failed flows from it.

SECTION 2 · CORE

The tokenomics machine

Steem's economy ran on three tokens, one inflation schedule, and one reward formula that was rewritten three times. Understanding these four pieces is understanding the whole system.

The three-token model

Most of the cleverness — and most of the confusion — lived in splitting "the token" into three instruments with different jobs:

TokenRoleKey property
STEEMThe liquid base asset. Tradeable, volatile, what you bought on exchanges.Liquid; inflationary. Holding it raw meant getting diluted.
Steem Power (SP)STEEM that's been "powered up" / vested. This is what votes. Voting influence ∝ SP.Illiquid — a 13-week linear power-down to exit (originally a punishing 2-year vest). Designed to align voters with the long term.4
Steem Dollars (SBD)A stable-token "backed" by STEEM, targeting ~$1, meant to give creators a non-volatile payout option.A debt instrument, convertible to ~$1 of STEEM. The peg held loosely at best. (mechanics: estimate)
Honest caveat: the deep research could not verify the exact SBD peg/conversion mechanics or the magnitude of its depeg episodes from primary sources. The general record is that SBD swung far above $1 during the 2017–18 bull run (briefly into the multiples of dollars) and below it later — i.e. it depegged in both directions — but treat specific SBD figures here as unverified.

The inflation engine

From December 2016 the supply grew at a predictable 9.5% per year, decreasing by 0.01% roughly every 8–9 days toward a 0.95% floor reached after ~20.5 years. Every newly minted token was split three ways:3

75%
→ rewards pool
Paid out to authors & curators by vote weight. The prize everyone competed for.
15%
→ SP holders
An interest-like yield to vested stakers, partly offsetting dilution.
10%
→ witnesses
Block producers securing the DPoS chain.

The structural tension is visible immediately: if you held liquid STEEM and did nothing, inflation diluted you. The system pushed everyone to either power up and vote, or sell. Passive holding was punished by design.

The reward curve — rewritten three times

The single most consequential parameter was the shape of the function mapping vote weight to payout. Steem changed it repeatedly, and each change is a confession about the previous one:

n³ → n²
launch → Jul 2016
Superlinear "anti-sybil" curve. Rewarded consolidating stake; explicitly discouraged splitting into small accounts.6
linear (n)
Jun 2017 · HF19
"Equality." One Steem of vote = the same payout regardless of who casts it. A direct admission that n² was "not fair."7
n²/(n+1)
Aug 2019 · HF21
"Convergent linear" — superlinear for small votes, linear for large. An attempt to punish dust/spam without re-enabling whale dominance.9

An engineer on the project put the n² problem bluntly: it was a "rich get richer" curve, and "certainly this is not fair. Steemit and the witnesses agree, which is why we changed the rewards curve to linear."7 The whiplash between curves is itself a finding: the team kept discovering that any curve, given stake-weighted voting underneath it, gets gamed — the curve only changes how.

The author / curator split

Within each post, the reward was divided between the author and the people who upvoted it ("curators"). This split was a tunable protocol parameter (percent_curation_rewards): curators collectively took a fixed slice, the author got the rest, with a ~30-minute reverse-auction redirecting some curation reward back to early-vs-late voters.5 The baseline started at 75% author / 25% curator.

In 2019, HF21 doubled curation to 50%. The stated logic is revealing: "the more rewards are distributed to curators the less incentive there is to self-vote."8 They were no longer designing for an ideal — they were re-tuning the dial to make the dominant abuse slightly less profitable.

SECTION 3 · UNDER THE HOOD

Infrastructure: storage, latency & the centralization underneath

A social network where every upvote is a blockchain write has two brutal engineering problems: where do you put the content, and how do you make the feed fast. Steem's answers to both were genuinely clever — and both quietly leaned on centralized infrastructure, which is the same vulnerability the governance crisis later exploited, seen from below.

Where content lived

Steem made an unusual call: it stored the actual text of every post and comment on-chain, inside the comment operation — not a hash, not a pointer, the full body.24 Media was pushed off-chain.

ContentWhere it livedImplication
Post & comment textOn-chain, in the comment op (title, body, json_metadata)Permanent, immutable, replicated on every full node. No true delete.
ImagesOff-chain CDN — Steemit ran steemitimages.com (live ~Feb 2017)One company hosting the media. Centralization vector.
Video (DTube)IPFS (base chain stored only the reference)The dApp layer chased decentralized storage; the core chain didn't.

On-chain text made posts censorship-resistant and permanent — but also un-deletable (a right-to-be-forgotten problem) and heavy: a full node carrying all content state was expensive to run, so most dApps just pointed at Steemit Inc's public nodes. The "decentralized" network had a centralized infrastructure spine.

Who paid to store it — stake-gated writes

There were no transaction fees, so two mechanisms stopped the free, permanent storage from being spammed:

Supply side

Witnesses are paid to persist it

Block producers replicate and store the chain, funded by the 10% of inflation routed to witnesses. The storers are paid by the protocol, not per-write fees.20

Demand side

Writing is gated by stake

You couldn't pay per post — instead your right to write was rationed by Steem Power. More SP = more capacity to write to permanent storage. No stake, no posting.

The gating mechanism evolved. Originally a "bandwidth" allowance proportional to SP; HF20 "Velocity" (Sept 2018) replaced it with Resource Credits (RCs) — non-transferable, mana-like credits granted by SP, regenerating over time and consumed per operation (a post costs more than a vote).23

The onboarding trap — ties straight to the retention problem: a brand-new account with zero SP had no credits and literally could not post. Early on, Steemit Inc had to delegate SP to bootstrap new accounts; HF20 shifted this to RC-based "claimed account" creation. Either way, the "anyone can earn by posting" pitch hit a "you need stake before you can post" wall at step one — and HF20's launch infamously froze many low-SP users out of posting for days while RC allocations were recalibrated.

How they handled latency

"Latency" was really two problems with two different architectures:

Write path — the 3-second block

The chain produced a block every 3 seconds (65 KB max) via DPoS. Speed came almost for free because the producer set is known and scheduled: ~21 witnesses (20 elected + 1 rotating "timeshare" slot) produce in a deterministic round-robin, each knowing exactly when its turn comes — no mining race, no propagation lottery.20 A laggy witness just gets its slot skipped (a "missed block," a 3-second gap, not a stall) and accumulates a missed-block count that gets it voted out.

3 s
block time
Inclusion latency. Optimistic UI showed your post/vote the instant it landed.
~45–60 s
irreversibility
A block is final once 16 of 21 witnesses (75%) build on it — the "last irreversible block."20
felt: 3 s
perceived
Only transfers/payouts waited for finality; the feed didn't, so it felt instant.

Read path — the part most people miss

Fast blocks don't give you a fast feed. Loading a trending page or a comment thread is a query, and you can't serve social reads by scanning a blockchain. Steem's unsung hero was an off-chain indexing stack:

LayerJob
HivemindThe "second-layer" social indexer. Ingests chain operations into a PostgreSQL database and serves feeds, threads, follows, communities and trending — reads hit Postgres, not the chain.21
JussiSteemit's custom reverse-proxy & caching layer, routing each request to the right backend (steemd vs Hivemind) and caching responses so repeat reads never touch the DB.22
CondenserThe steemit.com front-end, talking to the cached API — so it behaved like a normal web app.

So the real model was: writes go through the 3-second chain; reads are served from a cached Postgres index. That split is exactly what let it feel like Web2 instead of a block explorer.

Low latency and the single-point-of-dependency were the same decision, viewed from two angles. Hivemind, Jussi and the public API nodes were heavy to run, so most dApps used Steemit Inc's. The snappy UX and the centralization came from the same place.

This is the cleanest rebuttal to "just put social media on a blockchain." Steem proved the writes can be fast and feeless — but the moment a feed has to load quickly, you're back to a Postgres database and a CDN. Web2 infrastructure, wearing a blockchain underneath.

SECTION 4

What genuinely worked

A post-mortem that only catalogs failure misreads the thing. Steem was a real and influential experiment, and several of its bets paid off — or at least proved the concept was buildable.

Pioneered

Reusable creator-economy primitives

Rewards pool + community curation + vesting stake + a stable payout token + a parameterized author/curator split. Steem invented this kit. It later generalized into Smart Media Tokens — any project could carry built-in "Proof-of-Brain."13

Real money

Creators actually got paid

In the early days the payouts were real and sometimes large. For a window, "post and get crypto" was not a slogan — it happened, and it pulled in a genuine community of writers, photographers and developers.

Live ecosystem

A working dApp layer

It wasn't a single site. Splinterlands (a blockchain card game that became one of crypto's most-used dApps), DTube (decentralized video), Busy, eSteem and others built on the same social graph and token.1718

Infra

Fast, feeless UX

DPoS gave 3-second blocks and no per-action fees — non-negotiable for social media, where charging gas per upvote would be fatal. The chain handled hundreds of millions of operations.9

Studied

It became a research template

Steem is now a canonical case study — an HBS case, multiple peer-reviewed papers, and an 8-step "token economy design" framework all use it. Its failures are legible because everything happened on a public ledger.1219

Resilience

The community could exit

The 2020 Hive fork — escaping a hostile acquirer by forking the chain and airdropping a clean token — is, perversely, a success story: proof that an aligned community can route around a captured protocol.16

SECTION 5

Failure modes

Every failure below is downstream of one decision: votes are weighted by money, and money buys a share of a fixed reward pool. That turns "curation" into "yield farming," and the rest follows mechanically.

Plutocracy, measured

This isn't a vibe — it's in the data. A peer-reviewed study of 539 million operations by 1.12 million users (2016–2018) concluded the "actual level of decentralization in Steemit is far lower than the ideal," and that DPoS "may not be a desirable approach for establishing a highly decentralized social media platform."9 Stake concentration meant a small number of whales effectively decided what got paid.

Curation became a money game, not a taste game

A 2022 PLOS ONE study found that high-stake central users ("hubs") preferred voting to creating — because once you hold enough Steem Power, curating is simply more profitable than writing. The result: rewards showed "an absence of correlation with the creation factor." The platform paid for stake, not for content.11

Precision note: the "near-zero correlation between content creation and rewards" finding is firmest for top-decile users; one verifier flagged that generalizing it to all users slightly overreaches. The direction is solid; the universality is not.

The bid-bot economy

Because votes were money and money was a fixed pool, a market formed to buy votes. You'd send STEEM/SBD to a "bid-bot," which upvoted your post in proportion — often netting the buyer more in rewards than they paid in. The same 539M-operation study found more than 16% of transfers went to curators suspected to be bots, with an identifiable supply network behind them.10 Self-voting and "circle-jerk" voting rings did the same thing without the marketplace.

These practices undermine one of the core unique value propositions of the platform: proof-of-brain. — Steemit Inc, "Improving the Economics of Steem" (2019). The team naming its own death spiral.8

The doom loop

Quality

Content farming

When payout tracks stake and vote-buying rather than merit, low-effort content optimized for upvote-trading crowds out the good stuff. Discovery degrades.

Retention

Ghost-town dynamics

New users with no stake had near-zero influence and near-zero earnings. The promise ("get paid to post") was structurally false for the people it was marketed to, so they left.

Holders

Inflation dilution

~9.5% annual inflation meant passive holders bled value continuously, forcing everyone into the vote-game or the exit — accelerating both gaming and sell pressure.3

Peg

SBD instability

The "stable" dollar token didn't stay stable, undercutting its purpose as a safe payout. (magnitude unverified)

SECTION 6 · GOVERNANCE

The takeover that broke it open

All of the above was slow decay. What made Steem the textbook case in crypto governance was a fast, public, and almost theatrical demonstration that stake-weighted voting can be bought outright.

The latent flaw was the "ninja-mined" founder stake. Steem's early mining had concentrated a very large block of tokens — commonly cited at ~20% of supply — with the founders and Steemit Inc. For years it sat as an implicit promise not to be used to dominate governance. That promise was only as good as whoever owned it.

In February 2020, Justin Sun's Tron Foundation acquired Steemit Inc — and with it, that stake. HBS's case notes he thereby gained "potentially outsized influence over blockchain governance."14 The community read it as an existential threat and responded fast:

The freeze
Soft fork 0.22.2 — the existing witnesses moved to neutralize the Steemit stake's voting power, parking it.15
The counter
Sun called in favors. Binance, Huobi and Poloniex staked their customers' deposited STEEM and voted with it — enough to evict the entire top-20 witness set and install Sun-aligned ones. Using user funds for governance was the scandal within the scandal.15
The verdict
Vitalik Buterin labeled it "the first big instance of a de facto bribe attack on coin voting" — instantly a reference point in every later DAO-governance debate.15
The exit
Hive — a hard fork cloning the chain and state, airdropping HIVE to every holder except Sun's stake and the accounts that backed him. The community took the social graph, the dApps, and the culture; Sun kept the brand and an emptied chain.16

Two of the three exchanges (notably Binance) reportedly backed out once users realized their deposits had been weaponized — but the point was already made. Coin-voting is capturable the moment a large liquid stake exists and custodians can be persuaded to point it somewhere.

SECTION 7

Aftermath & verdict

The March 2020 fork cleaved the world in two. Hive inherited the active community, most of the dApps (Splinterlands migrated, DTube went multi-chain), and the moral high ground. Steem retained Justin Sun, the Steemit brand, and a chain the original community had largely abandoned. Both still exist.

Current-state caveat: the research did not pull verified 2026 figures for relative activity, token values, or surviving-dApp metrics on Hive vs Steem. Treat the "who's winning now" question as open — the verified record runs solidly through the 2020 split, not past it.

The verdict. Steem was simultaneously a success and a failure, and the two are inseparable. It succeeded at proving you can monetize social media at the protocol layer, build a real dApp ecosystem on a social token, and even let a community escape a hostile takeover by forking. It failed at the only thing that ultimately mattered: designing incentives where rewarding stake and rewarding merit point in the same direction. They never did, and no amount of curve-tuning could make money-weighted voting behave like taste-weighted voting.

SECTION 8

Transferable lessons

The reason this post-mortem is worth writing in 2026: every one of these failure modes recurs in points programs, creator rev-shares, social tokens, and DAO governance. Steem ran the experiment in public so the rest of us don't have to.

01

If you weight rewards by stake, you are building a yield product, not a meritocracy

"Curation" weighted by money is just farming with extra steps. Participants optimize for the payout function, not your stated goal. Steem said it was paying for brains; it was paying for stake, and the data proved it.

Apply: design the reward function as if every participant is an adversary maximizing it — because they are.

02

A fixed reward pool is an attack surface

The moment rewards come from a shared, gameable pool, a secondary market to extract from it (bid-bots, vote rings, sybil farms) is not a risk — it's an inevitability. >16% of transfers went to suspected bots.

Apply: in any points/rev-share program, model the extraction market before launch and assume it will be industrialized.

03

You cannot patch a structural flaw with parameter tweaks

Three reward curves, a doubled curation split, a DAO — each was a real attempt, each shifted behavior without curing it, because the disease (money-weighted voting) was upstream of every dial they could turn.

Apply: when you find yourself re-tuning the same parameter repeatedly, the bug is in the model, not the constant.

04

Coin-voting governance is capturable — custody is the back door

Sun didn't need to buy a majority; he borrowed voting power from exchanges holding customer funds. Any system where a large liquid stake exists and a custodian can mobilize it is one phone call from capture.

Apply: for any on-chain vote, ask "what happens if an exchange votes its users' deposits?" If the answer is catastrophic, redesign. Relevant to any prediction-market or token system contemplating stake-weighted governance.

05

A concentrated founder stake is a time bomb on a long fuse

The ninja-mined stake sat harmlessly for years as a gentleman's agreement — until it was sold to someone who hadn't agreed. Allocations outlive intentions.

Apply: treat any large pre-mine/treasury as if it will eventually be held by your worst-case adversary, because ownership transfers and norms don't.

06

Punishing passive holding forces everyone into the casino

~9.5% inflation meant doing nothing lost you money, pushing every holder into either gaming the pool or selling. High inflation aimed at "engagement" can manufacture both the abuse and the sell pressure that kill you.

Apply: align the no-effort baseline with neutrality. If inertia is punished, you've conscripted your whole userbase into adversarial behavior.

07

The exit valve is a feature — design for forkability and clean airdrops

Hive worked because state was forkable and the community could airdrop a clean token that excluded the bad actor. The credible threat of exit is itself a governance check.

Apply: a community that can credibly leave has leverage a captive one never will. Build the door even if you hope it's never used.
